On Sunday, July 19th, 2026, the [US Embassy in Amman](https://x.com/USEmbassyJordan/status/2078777693370200223) said Jordanian authorities had evacuated Aqaba’s international airport and seaport because of a “specific and credible threat.” It told US citizens not to travel to either location and to follow Jordanian security instructions.

Jordan’s government pushed back the same day. Government spokesman Mohammad Al-Momani said relevant authorities had **not** issued evacuation orders for Aqaba’s airport or seaport, that both were operating normally, and that officials had not identified potential threats to Aqaba in recent hours. That denial was also amplified through Jordanian channels, including a [Ministry of Government Communications post recirculated by Al-Mamlaka](https://x.com/AlMamlakaTV/status/2078785762527436977) (from [@MoGC_JO](https://x.com/MoGC_JO)).

That gap (US alert vs Jordanian denial) is what matters most for travelers having to deal with conflicting official messages on the same day.

Aqaba is where a lot of Red Sea diving trips, Egypt ferries, and southern Jordan road trips go through, so an airport or seaport alert there could affect travelers’ itineraries.

## What this means for travelers

If you are flying into or out of **King Hussein International Airport (AQJ)**, using the **Aqaba seaport**, or overnighting in Aqaba in the next few days, check multiple sources and decide with what you find. Sunday’s messages are not proof the airport is closed, and not proof the alert was meaningless.

Practical order of operations:

1. Confirm your **flight status with the airline** (not only social media screenshots).
2. Read **more than one country’s advisory** (Canada, US, UK, France, and others) **plus Jordanian local reporting and official channels**. Most of the time those pages line up. When they differ, go with your risk tolerance. The US is deeply involved in the region, so its alert may be worth taking seriously. If you are not there yet, the conservative read is often the safer default when in those types of situations; if you are already on the ground, assess with local information too.
3. If you are already in Aqaba, a quick check with your hotel or dive operator is useful, mostly so you hear what they are seeing too. If you are not there yet, but are still coming, it can also be useful to contact them in those cases to let them know you are still coming.
4. Keep avoiding **military facilities** and large crowds if your embassy already advises that, which has been standard US guidance in Jordan through this conflict period.

The operational question (is AQJ/port actually evacuated?) is answered first by Jordanian authorities and people on site. After you have looked at multiple sources, the call is yours.

## Why the alert landed now

On July 17th, 2026, [US Central Command](https://www.centcom.mil/MEDIA/PUBLIC-RELEASES/Article/4549635/centcom-statement-on-recently-fallen-missing-us-service-members/) said two US service members in Jordan were killed in action while CENTCOM and partner forces defended against Iranian ballistic missile and drone attacks, and that one service member was missing. CENTCOM also said four service members were medically evacuated to Jordanian hospitals and later discharged.

**In Jordan**, that is a sharper military casualty picture than many travelers associated with earlier months of this conflict (even though other countries saw serious casualties in earlier phases). It can help explain why a US embassy can publish hard risk language quickly, and why you should still get a same-day reality check from Jordanian officials, your airline, and local operators when those messages aren't in line.

## What to do if you have Jordan plans

**Already booked through AMM (Queen Alia) or AQJ:**  
Keep the booking, monitor the airline, and re-check in the days before travel. Amman and Aqaba are not the same situation. A threat message aimed at Aqaba’s airport/port is not the same as a nationwide “Jordan is closed” claim. Monitor general airspace for closures. That was the main concern when I was there earlier in this conflict.

**Heading to Aqaba for diving or the coast:**  
At this level of alert noise, things are very likely running. Still worth staying in touch with your dive shop or hotel so they know you have not cancelled on them. Tourism businesses have been dealing with a lot of drop-offs, and a short message helps them plan. If you are especially anxious, ask what they are seeing on the ground, but do not assume everything is shut because of one contested alert.

**Flexible dates:**  
If your trip is optional and the alerts escalate beyond a contested one-day message, it can make sense to wait. What we can say from experience is in the section below, and that trip was before the July casualties, so still re-check today’s facts.

## On the ground: what 5.5 weeks in Jordan looked like earlier this year

I was in Jordan from **February 21st to April 2nd, 2026** (Canadian passport; my partner is American). We flew in and out of **Amman (AMM)**. We spent **March 22nd to March 25th in Aqaba**, including a Red Sea dive on March 24th, after Petra and Wadi Rum, plus more time in Amman and at the Dead Sea.

The war started while we were there. We heard sirens often enough, and a few times we could see interceptions overhead. We still did not feel unsafe as tourists. Most of our different guides’ take was basically: Jordan stays open for visitors, and people here are used to living next to regional crises, but rarely get involved. Locals depending on tourism were mostly sad about cancelled trips and empty hotels. They were still happy to see the tourists who showed up.

The scary version of Jordan from abroad did not match what we saw day to day. We did not run into a bunch of new checkpoints. We got embassy alerts, but they were often late or not that useful next to local news. We had been told to avoid US base areas. We still drove past one on a tour to see some castles in the east and did not notice anything extra.

That was **before** the July wave with confirmed US deaths in Jordan. Sirens and interceptions can look familiar and still not mean the risk is the same. My trip report can make Jordan sound manageable, and it was when I was there, but re-check **today’s** CENTCOM, embassy, and Jordanian updates before you copy our itinerary.

What we did practically: always know where the passports are, and carry more cash than usual in case you need to leave in a hurry (which we didn't need to do).

## Bottom line

Sunday came down to a **US Embassy Amman alert** describing an Aqaba airport/seaport evacuation over a “specific and credible threat,” and **Jordan’s government** saying **no evacuation was ordered** and both were operating normally. What you should do in those cases: check your flight with the airline, read more than one country’s advisory, weigh Jordanian official and local reporting, then decide.
