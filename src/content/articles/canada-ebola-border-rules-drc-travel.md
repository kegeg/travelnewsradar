On July 19th, 2026, the [Public Health Agency of Canada](https://www.canada.ca/en/public-health/news/2026/07/new-temporary-border-measures-in-response-to-the-ebola-disease-outbreak.html) announced new temporary border measures tied to the Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC).

As of **Monday, July 20th, 2026, at 11:59 p.m. EDT**, any **foreign national who was in the DRC in the past 21 days** is prohibited from entering Canada. An Interim Order under the Aeronautics Act also tells commercial and private air carriers not to board those travelers on flights to Canada.

That sits on top of earlier [Quarantine Act measures](https://www.canada.ca/en/public-health/services/diseases/ebola/border-measures.html) that started May 30th and run through **August 29th, 2026**. Under those rules, travelers who were in the **DRC, Uganda, or South Sudan** in the previous 21 days, including Canadian citizens, permanent residents, persons registered under the Indian Act, **and foreign nationals who are still allowed to enter**, get a health assessment on arrival and must quarantine for 21 days. After July 20th, foreign nationals with recent **DRC** time are barred outright, so the quarantine path mainly still applies to Canadians/PRs coming from the DRC, and to anyone eligible coming from Uganda or South Sudan. PHAC says the health risk to people in Canada remains low, and that no travel-related Ebola cases have been reported in Canada so far.

## What this means for travelers

If you are flying to Canada soon, the new rule is mainly about **who was physically in the DRC in the last three weeks**, not about your passport country alone.

Practical checks:

1. Look at your **last 21 days of travel history**, not just your final departure city. A connection through Kinshasa or time on the ground in the DRC can matter even if your ticket says you are “coming from” somewhere else.
2. If you are a **foreign national** and you were in the **DRC** in that window, assume you **cannot board** a flight to Canada under the new measure. Confirm with your airline before you go to the airport.
3. If you are a **Canadian citizen or PR** returning after time in the DRC, Uganda, or South Sudan, you can still enter under the current Quarantine Act rules, but plan for **assessment plus 21 days of quarantine** through August 29th (unless the measures change). Same quarantine frame applies to **foreign nationals** who are still allowed in after Uganda or South Sudan time.
4. Re-check official government pages close to travel. These orders get extended and rewritten frequently. Canada’s own border-measures page also notes that **transit/refueling** through the listed countries can still count.

## How this compares with the US

The United States has had CDC entry measures in place since May over the same outbreak. In short: **many foreign nationals** who were in the **DRC, Uganda, or South Sudan** in the past 21 days have been blocked from entering the US, while **US citizens and nationals** can still enter with enhanced public health screening. The CDC has extended that framework into mid-August 2026 (see [CDC guidance for travelers returning from affected areas](https://www.cdc.gov/viral-hemorrhagic-fevers/travel-to-us/index.html) and the related [entry-order page](https://www.cdc.gov/port-health/legal-authorities/evdorder.html)). Confirm the current end date before you book, because it has already been extended more than once.

If your plan is only “transit” a country, ask whether that means **airside connection** or clearing immigration. Rules are often harder once you enter. For the US specifically, most connecting itineraries mean you enter.

## Europe and other hubs

The EU has not matched the US/Canada-style entry bans. In May, the EU Health Security Committee said screening and other specific travel measures for arrivals from the DRC and Uganda were **not** needed at that stage ([HSC opinion, May 22nd, 2026](https://health.ec.europa.eu/document/download/f4d25a0d-b3bb-4205-a0ea-f138acd23027_en?filename=security_ebola-2026_opinion_en.pdf)). That can change at any moment.

Some European countries still have paperwork. Italy, for example, has required arrivals from the DRC and Uganda (or presence there in the prior 21 days) to file a health declaration with local authorities after entry. Other regions, including some Middle East hubs, announced temporary suspensions earlier in this outbreak. If your flights go through a third country, check **that** country’s rules too, not only Canada and the US.

## What to do if you have Africa or Canada plans

**Already booked into Canada after recent DRC time (foreign passport):**  
Talk to the airline now. Under the July measure, boarding to Canada may be refused.

**Canadian returning after DRC, Uganda, or South Sudan:**  
Keep the trip only if you can actually quarantine for 21 days. Have a plan before you land.

**Planning South Sudan or Uganda later this year:**  
Current US and Canada end dates are in August, which is before many late-year trips. Do not assume the rules will be gone by then. Assume they might be extended, and you probably should not buy non-refundable long-haul tickets until you re-check the orders.

**Flexible routing:**  
If US or Canadian connections are optional, also check for Europe, Middle East, and African hubs as well. The “best” ticket on points or cash is useless if someone on the booking cannot enter under their nationality.

## On the road: why we’re watching this for a South Sudan trip

Caroline and I are planning a **South Sudan** trip later this year, with a little time in **Uganda** afterward. Nothing is booked yet other than the deposit for the trip. We are holding the long-haul tickets and will decide closer to departure. This is why these border updates matter to us even when we are not going specifically to the DRC.

Right now the rough plan is to fly into the region from Asia via the Middle East, and then fly out through Uganda (that is currently where the cheaper outbound flights are), then onward to South America. A lot of Atlantic options have a **US** connection. I am Canadian; Caroline is American. Under the current CDC frame, after South Sudan or Uganda I would be the one who would get stuck if we needed to enter the US. On the Canada side, her American passport would not be hit by the new foreign-national **DRC** ban after South Sudan alone, but she could still face the **21-day quarantine** if we entered Canada after Uganda or South Sudan while those May–August measures are still in force. After recent DRC time, she would be in the banned foreign-national bucket.

We would rather avoid both the US and Canada on the way out if we can. Europe has not copied the hard bans so far, which helps, but we still have to check whatever hub we actually use, including paperwork if we clear immigration somewhere like Italy (unlikely). If more countries pile into entry bans, we may scrub the trip. For now it stays on the calendar while we monitor the situation.

## Bottom line

Canada’s July update adds a hard line: **foreign nationals who were in the DRC in the past 21 days cannot enter Canada**, and airlines must not board them, starting late July 20th. Older quarantine rules for returns after DRC, Uganda, or South Sudan still run through late August for people who are allowed in. The US list for many non-citizens is broader and already includes South Sudan and Uganda. Check your last 21 days, the rules for everyone traveling on the booking, and every country on the ticket before you pay.
