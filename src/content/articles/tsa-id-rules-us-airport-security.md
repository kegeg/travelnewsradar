The TSA is tightening which forms of identification clear US airport security without extra screening friction. Most travelers with a current REAL ID–compliant driver’s license or a valid passport will notice little change. People still carrying older non-compliant licenses are the group that needs a plan.

Airlines still fly you; the checkpoint is where the rule shows up. That split is why travelers get blindsided at security after a normal online check-in.

## What this means for travelers

If you only fly domestically inside the United States, confirm your license is REAL ID–compliant before your next trip. If it is not, bring an acceptable alternative (passport is the simplest) rather than arguing policy at the belt.

Canadian and other international visitors using a passport already meet the common path. The sharper edge case is US residents who never updated their state ID and assumed boarding pass alone was enough.

## Quick checklist before you leave for the airport

- Photo ID that meets current TSA standards
- Matching name on ticket and ID
- Extra 20–30 minutes at larger airports while enforcement habits settle

## If your ID might fail

Do not wait until the queue. Check TSA’s current accepted ID list, renew if needed, or travel with a passport book. A cheap fix at home beats a missed flight.
